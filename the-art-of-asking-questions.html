The Art of Asking Questions

Published on October 27, 2023

It occurred to me the other day that we spend most of our early lives being taught how to answer questions, and almost no time learning how to ask them. Yet, the question is the engine of thought.
A single, well-framed question can be more valuable than a dozen correct answers. It can open a door you didn't know was there, or gently close one that was leading you in circles.

I've come to see questions not as interrogations, but as invitations. An invitation to dig deeper, to see a familiar problem from a slightly different angle, to connect two ideas that seemed worlds apart. The classic "five whys" technique from manufacturing works just as well in the quiet of your own mind. Why am I stuck on this paragraph? Because I'm not sure of the core message. Why am I not sure? Because I haven't defined it clearly for myself. And with each successive "why," you drill past the superficial symptoms and touch the real root.

The most powerful questions are often the simplest, and the most courageous. "What if I'm wrong?" isn't a admission of failure; it's a declaration of intellectual honesty. "What does this remind me of?" can weave a thread between a childhood memory and a modern-day challenge, revealing a pattern you would have otherwise missed. What if we started our day not with a to-do list, but with a 'to-ask' list? Not questions for others, necessarily, but for ourselves and for the day ahead.

  • What small thing can I observe today that I usually overlook?
  • What assumption am I making that I could productively challenge?
  • Who can I learn from, if I'm just quiet enough to listen?

The to-do list prescribes action. The to-ask list cultivates a state of mind‚Äîone of active curiosity. And I've found, after all these years, that a curious mind, fueled by good questions, tends to find its way to the right actions all on its own.
The answers become almost secondary; the real discovery is in the asking.
