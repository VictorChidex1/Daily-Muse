The Courage of a Changed Mind

Published on November 17, 2023

We are raised to value consistency. We are told to "stand our ground," to "stick to our principles." In the public sphere, changing your mind is often portrayed as a "flip-flop," a sign of weakness, a lack of conviction. We build our identities around our beliefs, and to change a belief can feel like a betrayal of the self. So we cling to our positions long after the evidence has shifted beneath us.

But I would argue that the ability to change your mind is one of the highest forms of intellectual courage. It requires humility to admit that your current understanding might be incomplete or incorrect. It requires strength to prioritize the truth over your own ego. A mind that is open to change is not a weak mind; it is a growing mind. It is a living, adaptive ecosystem. A mind that never changes is a stagnant pond, destined for inertia.

The process of changing your mind is not an event, but a journey. It often starts with a niggling doubt, a piece of data that doesn't fit your existing model. The courageous individual leans into that discomfort. They seek out contrary viewpoints not to defeat them, but to understand them. They ask, "What would it take for me to believe the opposite? What evidence would convince me?" This is not being disloyal to your old idea; it is being loyal to the truth.

I have had to change my mind about significant things: about career paths, about parenting strategies, about long-held artistic tastes. Each time, it was initially unsettling. It felt like the floor was moving. But on the other side of that discomfort was always a feeling of expansion, of seeing the world with greater clarity and complexity. My identity is not a fortress to be defended at all costs, but a tree, growing, shedding old leaves, and putting out new branches in the direction of the light. The goal is not to be right forever, but to be less wrong over time. And that requires the ongoing, courageous work of being willing to see anew.
