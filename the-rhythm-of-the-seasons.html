The Rhythm of the Seasons

Published on October 20, 2025

Modernity has placed us in a state of perpetual, climate-controlled summer. Our food, our work, our entertainment are available in a constant, undifferentiated stream, 24/7/365. We have insulated ourselves from the most fundamental rhythm of the natural world: the cycle of the seasons. And in doing so, I believe we have divorced ourselves from a deep, organic wisdom about our own energy and creativity.

Nature doesn't operate at a constant, frantic pace. It pulses. Spring is a burst of explosive, almost chaotic growth, a time for new ideas, for planting seeds, for bold beginnings. Summer is a time of consolidation and thriving, of nurturing those seeds, of focused effort, of enjoying the sun. Autumn is a time of release and harvest and of reaping what we've sown, of letting go of what is no longer needed, of gathering resources. Winter is a time of deep rest and introspection, of stillness, of hidden growth beneath the surface, of conservation and planning.

Now, look at your own life, your own work. We are taught that we must be in a state of eternal, high-output summer. We feel guilty for our 'winters', our periods of low energy, creative block, or the need for rest. We see them as failures. But what if they are not? What if they are a necessary, vital part of the cycle? A field that is never allowed to lie fallow becomes exhausted and barren. So does a human being.

I've started to map my own projects and energy levels to this seasonal rhythm. There are times for spring-like brainstorming and enthusiastic launches. There are times for summer's diligent, heads-down work. There are times for autumn's editing, refining, and shipping. And I am learning, slowly and with great difficulty, to grant myself permission for winter, for the fallow time, for the quiet reflection, for the seemingly unproductive period where the next big idea is germinating in the dark soil of the subconscious. By aligning our efforts with this natural rhythm, instead of fighting a futile war against it, we find a more sustainable, graceful, and ultimately more fruitful way to live and create.
