The Unlikely Connections

Published on November 2, 2023

We are taught to specialize, to dive deep into a single silo of knowledge. Our education systems, our career ladders, all reinforce the idea of the expert who knows everything about one thing. But I am increasingly convinced that the most profound breakthroughs‚Äîthe ideas that truly change our personal lives and our world‚Äîdo not come from diving deeper into a single well. They come from building aqueducts between different wells.

Creativity, at its heart, is not a act of genesis from a void. It is the act of connection. It is the ability to see a relationship between two seemingly unrelated fields and forge a new link. The classic example is how Steve Jobs connected the aesthetics of calligraphy he audited at Reed College with the interface of the first Macintosh, fundamentally changing how humans interact with computers. It was the connection between biology and architecture that gave us biomimicry‚Äîbuildings that cool themselves like termite mounds.

Our minds are vast, silent libraries, but we often keep the books in separate, locked sections. The "Science" wing never talks to the "Art" wing. The "Personal Experience" archive is kept far from the "Business Strategy" department. What happens when we give ourselves permission to be the librarian who reshelves the entire collection? When we take a principle from mycology‚Äîthe study of fungi and their underground networks‚Äîand apply it to building a community? Or use the narrative arc of a classic hero's journey to structure a project plan?

I practice this through a "Commonplace Book" of sorts, where I jot down interesting concepts from wildly different domains. A quote from a physicist next to an observation from a poet. A lesson from a video game next to a theory from psychology. I then deliberately spend time looking for echoes and patterns. I ask forced questions: "How is this marketing problem like a river ecosystem?" "What would this relationship dynamic look like as a mathematical equation?"

The space between disciplines is not a no-man's land of irrelevance. It is the most fertile, unexplored territory for innovation. It requires courage to be an amateur, to be a dilettante in the best sense of the word. But the reward is a unique perspective, the ability to see the patterns that connect the world, and the chance to create something that is not just new, but truly novel.
